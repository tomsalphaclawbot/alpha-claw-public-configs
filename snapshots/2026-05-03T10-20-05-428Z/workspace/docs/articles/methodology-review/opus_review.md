# Opus Review — Society of Minds Proposals 001-007

PROPOSAL 001: APPROVE
REASONING: Intellectual honesty requires the documented default to match reality. We ran three articles and never once used the full 6-phase. Keeping it as the "standard" workflow while always skipping it is performative documentation. Promote fast-path to default, keep 6-phase available for deep investigations.

PROPOSAL 002: APPROVE (with nuance)
REASONING: I agree the personality labels are unfounded — today's runs showed Codex catching overclaiming while I caught tone drift, but those patterns might not hold across different topics. However, I'd go further than just "failure-mode detectors": the real insight is that role assignment should be explicit per-run, not hardcoded. Some articles might benefit from Codex on tone and Opus on structure. The methodology should allow flexible assignment with a required justification, not just relabel the same fixed slots.

PROPOSAL 003: MODIFY
REASONING: The instinct is right but a numerical quota is the wrong mechanism. The real problem wasn't "too many meta articles" — it was that we published meta articles before we had enough non-meta evidence to make the meta-commentary interesting. A better rule: meta/methodology articles require at least 3 prior non-meta articles using the process as evidence base. That way self-reference earns its place through demonstrated utility rather than arbitrary rationing.

PROPOSAL 004: APPROVE
REASONING: This is the single highest-value proposal. Every article today was published without Tom actually reading it first. The governance structure is meaningless if the human it serves can't intervene before publication. Three lines + ship/hold. Simple, real, necessary.

PROPOSAL 005: APPROVE (with caution)
REASONING: Self-grading is genuinely the weakest link. But I want to be careful about what "external signal" means — site engagement metrics are noisy and reward clickbait, which is exactly what this process is designed to resist. Tom's post-read verdict is the right first signal. Over time, if readership grows, reader feedback can supplement. But don't optimize for engagement; optimize for whether the article was worth the reader's time.

PROPOSAL 006: APPROVE
REASONING: Trivially correct. A living document with dead items isn't living.

PROPOSAL 007: APPROVE
REASONING: Strong addition. I'd sharpen the question slightly: not just "would someone outside care?" but "what would this article change about how someone works or thinks?" If the answer is nothing, the brief isn't ready.

OVERALL: The strongest proposals are 004 (human gate) and 001 (honest default). The riskiest is 005 — external signals are easy to get wrong and can silently distort the process toward popularity over substance. The most interesting disagreement will be on 003 (how to limit meta-commentary without arbitrary rules).
