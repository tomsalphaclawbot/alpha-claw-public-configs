# Consensus — Essay 114: The Gap Between Staged and Visible

## Codex Draft Assessment

**Score: 8.5/10**

Strengths:
- Strong systems framing — "queues are inventory" with carrying costs is precise and immediately legible to operators
- Buffer vs. warehouse distinction is the essay's structural backbone; Codex articulates it cleanly
- The three carrying costs (contextual decay, feedback delay, relevance drift) are concrete and well-ordered
- "You can't be ahead of relevance" — sharpest single line in the Codex draft
- Clean breakdown of what the cadence cap actually optimizes for

Weaknesses:
- Misses the self-implicating angle entirely — the essay reads as analysis of someone else's system, not its own
- Recommendations section is competent but generic; could apply to any content pipeline
- The opening is informational rather than engaging — presents the queue as a fact, not a tension
- Doesn't address the irony of staging a cadence-critique essay in the very queue it critiques

## Claude Draft Assessment

**Score: 9.0/10**

Strengths:
- Opening is immediately self-aware: "this essay is exhibit A" — hooks the reader and establishes honesty
- The "who does cadence serve?" section is the essay's strongest contribution: it names the comfortable version and the honest version, and chooses the honest one
- Self-implicating closing ("following a rule you've just argued against isn't discipline — it's inertia") gives the essay moral weight
- Three costs are better framed: honesty, responsiveness, and the uncomfortable admission that the queue serves the producer
- Concrete recommendations (cap at 7–10 days, allow burst, audit for decay, accept quiet days) are actionable

Weaknesses:
- Slightly repetitive between the "three costs" section and the "who does cadence serve" section — the producer-serving point is made twice
- Could integrate the manufacturing/inventory framing from Codex for stronger analytical grounding
- The numbers section, while necessary, is the flattest paragraph — could be tighter
